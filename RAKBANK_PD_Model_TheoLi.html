Dataset Exploration
Understanding the data before building anything

Class Imbalance in TARGET

The dataset contains 50,000 applicants, of whom 4,048 defaulted (8.1%). The remaining 91.9% repaid normally.

⚠️
This imbalance makes accuracy a misleading metric
A model that predicts "no default" for every applicant achieves 91.9% accuracy while catching zero actual defaulters. We therefore evaluate using AUC-ROC, Gini, and KS statistic, which measure how well the model separates defaulters from non-defaulters regardless of the threshold chosen. We also apply class_weight='balanced' during training, which tells the model to treat each defaulter as approximately 11 times more important than each non-defaulter, preventing it from ignoring the minority class.
Fig 1 — Class distribution (left) and missing value profile by column (right)

What Missing Bureau Information Implies

8,752 applicants (17.5%) are missing every bureau column simultaneously. This is not random data loss. It means these applicants have never borrowed before and therefore have no credit file with any bureau.

GroupCountDefault RateImplication
No bureau file8,7523.9%First-time borrowers with no credit history. Lower observed risk.
Has bureau file41,2489.0%Established borrowers. Higher default rate because past behaviour is tracked.
Business Implication
Applicants with no bureau file are not high risk, they are unknown risk. Auto-declining them would eliminate 17.5% of the applicant pool, many of whom are likely creditworthy. The appropriate response is to score them on available signals (income, employment, EXT_SOURCE) and, where risk is low, approve with a conservative initial credit limit.

Feature Engineering
Translating raw data into credit-relevant signals

Raw fields such as DAYS_BIRTH = -15,140 carry limited meaning on their own. The goal of feature engineering is to construct variables that directly reflect credit risk concepts a lender would recognise.

The features are organised into three dimensions. Each captures something the others do not: an applicant could be affordable on paper but have a poor repayment history, or be highly stable but over-leveraged. Together they give a complete picture.

AffordabilityCan this person sustain the repayments?
ANNUITY_TO_INCOME
AMT_ANNUITY / AMT_INCOME
Monthly repayment as a share of annual income. Values above 30% indicate meaningful affordability stress.
CREDIT_TO_INCOME
AMT_CREDIT / AMT_INCOME
Loan amount as a multiple of annual income. A loan worth 8 years of income represents fundamentally different risk from one worth 1 year, even at the same monthly repayment.
BUREAU_DEBT_TO_INCOME
BUREAU_TOTAL_DEBT / AMT_INCOME
Total existing debt across all lenders relative to income. An applicant may look affordable on this loan alone but if they already owe several times their annual income elsewhere, the combined burden is very different.
LOAN_COST_RATIO
(ANNUITY x TERM) / CREDIT
Total repayments divided by the original loan. This captures the interest cost that ANNUITY_TO_INCOME misses: two applicants with the same monthly payment but different loan terms have very different total burdens.
StabilityHow predictable is their financial situation?
AGE_YEARS
-DAYS_BIRTH / 365
Younger applicants tend to have shorter employment histories, less savings, and more volatile income. Age serves as a proxy for financial maturity.
EMPLOYMENT_YEARS
-DAYS_EMPLOYED / 365
Length of current employment is a direct indicator of income reliability. Someone employed for ten or more years is far less likely to experience sudden income loss than someone who started recently.
Credit HistoryWhat does past behaviour tell us?
BUREAU_TOTAL_DPD
DPD_30 + DPD_60 + DPD_90
Total count of all historical late payment events across all severity levels. Past payment behaviour is the single strongest predictor of future behaviour.
EXT_SOURCE_MEAN
mean(EXT_1, EXT_2, EXT_3)
Average of three independent external credit bureau scores. Each bureau assesses the applicant from a different angle. Combining them reduces single-source noise.
EXT_SOURCE_MIN
min(EXT_1, EXT_2, EXT_3)
The lowest of the three bureau scores. The mean can mask a red flag: if one agency scores an applicant very low, that matters regardless of the other two.
BUREAU_UTILIZATION
BUREAU_UTILIZATION (0 if missing)
Credit utilisation across existing bureau accounts. High utilisation indicates existing credit lines are nearly exhausted, a sign of financial stress.

Preprocessing Pipeline
Building a leakage-free, reproducible workflow

Stratified Train / Validation Split

The dataset is split 80/20 into a training set (40,000 applicants) and a validation set (10,000 applicants). The split is stratified on TARGET, meaning both sets preserve the 8.1% default rate. This ensures the validation set is representative and performance metrics are not distorted.

SplitSizeDefault RatePurpose
Training set40,0008.1%All preprocessing fitted here. Model trained here.
Validation set10,0008.1%Used only for evaluation. Never seen during training.

Why Preprocessing Must Be Fitted Only on Training Data

Preprocessing steps such as median imputation and categorical encoding require statistics computed from data, for example the median income used to fill missing values.

If these statistics are computed on the full 50,000-row dataset before splitting, the validation set's values have already shaped the preprocessing parameters. When the model is then evaluated on the validation set, it is being tested on data that has already influenced its inputs. The evaluation is no longer independent and performance metrics will be inflated.

Wrong — Causes Leakage
Compute imputation median on all 50,000 rows, then split. The validation set influenced the median the model uses. Performance metrics are inflated and do not reflect accuracy on genuinely unseen applicants.
Correct — No Leakage
Split first. Compute the imputation median on the 40,000 training rows only. Apply that same median to both sets. The validation set never influenced any parameter. Evaluation is honest.

This is enforced by wrapping all preprocessing inside a scikit-learn Pipeline. The Pipeline's fit() method sees only training data, and its transform() method applies learned parameters to both sets without re-fitting.

Model Training and Evaluation
Logistic Regression as the baseline model

Model Selection

Logistic Regression is the established baseline for credit scoring. It assigns a weight to each feature, sums the weighted values, and converts the result to a probability between 0 and 1. It is the appropriate starting point because its coefficients are directly interpretable and it is the format most familiar to credit risk practitioners and regulators.

Note that EXT_SOURCE_2 shows a positive coefficient due to multicollinearity among the three correlated EXT_SOURCE variables; the composite EXT_SOURCE_MEAN correctly shows a negative coefficient and should be treated as the primary signal.

From Coefficients to SHAP

The coefficient table shows the model's fixed weights, which are the same for every applicant. SHAP (in Section 5) goes further: it shows how much each feature actually contributed to a specific individual's prediction, taking into account their actual values. A feature with a large coefficient may have little impact on a particular applicant if their value for it is close to average.

Each dot in the chart below is one applicant. Red means a high value for that feature; blue means low. A dot sitting to the right means that feature increased that applicant's default probability; to the left means it reduced it.

Risk Bands and Lending Policy
Translating model scores into an Approve / Review / Decline framework

A continuous probability score is not directly actionable. The final step translates PD scores into three decision tiers. Thresholds are set at the 30th and 60th percentile of predicted scores, producing a 30/30/40 split that reflects the natural risk distribution in this portfolio. Each band is then validated against actual default outcomes.

The Decline band actual default rate (18.4%) is over 20 times higher than the Auto Approve band (0.8%). This separation confirms the model meaningfully differentiates risk levels and the thresholds produce genuinely distinct risk groups.

Policy Rationale

Auto Approve (bottom 30%): At a 0.8% actual default rate, this group sits well within standard retail credit risk appetite. Straight-through processing reduces cost and improves applicant experience without meaningful additional risk.

Manual Review (middle 30%): This band contains applicants where the model identifies material but not disqualifying risk. An underwriter can verify income documentation, consider collateral, or adjust the approved amount before making a final decision.

Decline (top 40%): At a 18.4% default rate, the expected loss from approving this group exceeds what standard risk appetite would allow. Applicants at the lower end of this band may be reconsidered for a reduced credit limit rather than an outright decline.
