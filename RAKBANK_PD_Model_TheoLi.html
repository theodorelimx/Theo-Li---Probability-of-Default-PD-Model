Executive Summary
What this model does and why it matters for Retail Lending

Every loan application is a decision under uncertainty. Approve a good customer and the bank earns interest income over the life of the loan. Approve a bad one and the bank absorbs the loss. With thousands of applications arriving each month, manual underwriting cannot be consistent, fast, or cost-effective enough to get every decision right.

This model addresses that challenge by scoring each applicant on their probability of default using three categories of signals: whether they can afford the repayments, how stable their financial situation is, and what their past borrowing behaviour looks like. It is trained on 40,000 historical applicants and validated on a separate 10,000 it had never seen.

The output is a three-tier decision framework. The 30% of applicants who are clearly low risk are approved automatically. The 40% who are clearly high risk are declined automatically. The remaining 30% who are genuinely borderline are routed to underwriters, who can focus their judgment where it adds the most value.

What This Means for the Lending Team
The model does not replace underwriter judgment. It concentrates that judgment on the cases where it genuinely matters. Underwriters spend their time on borderline applications where income verification, context around past delinquencies, and loan purpose assessment can change the outcome. They do not spend time on applications that are clearly approvable or clearly not.

Dataset Exploration
What the data tells us before we build anything

The Scale of the Problem

The dataset contains 50,000 loan applicants, of whom 4,048 defaulted, an 8.1% default rate. At that rate, a portfolio of 50,000 loans would generate over 4,000 defaults if approved without any risk filtering. The model's job is to identify those applicants before approval, not after.

⚠️
Why accuracy is the wrong metric here
A model that predicts "no default" for every applicant achieves 91.9% accuracy while catching zero actual defaulters. In lending terms, this model would approve everyone and offer no risk protection at all. We therefore evaluate using AUC-ROC, Gini, and KS statistic, which measure how well the model separates high-risk from low-risk applicants regardless of the threshold chosen.

The Thin-File Opportunity

8,752 applicants (17.5%) are missing every bureau column simultaneously. This is not a data quality issue. It means these applicants have never borrowed before and have no credit file on record. The instinct may be to decline them, but the data says otherwise.

GroupCountDefault RateBusiness Implication
No bureau file8,7523.9%First-time borrowers. Lower actual default rate than those with established credit histories.
Has bureau file41,2489.0%Established borrowers. Higher rate because past behaviour, including missed payments, is on record.
Recommendation: Do Not Auto-Decline Thin-File Applicants
Auto-declining applicants with no bureau file would eliminate 17.5% of the pipeline. These applicants are not high risk, they are unknown risk. The correct approach is to score them on available signals such as income, employment stability, and external credit scores, and where the model assigns low risk, approve with a conservative initial credit limit. A step-up review after 6 to 12 months of observed repayment behaviour allows the limit to be adjusted based on actual performance.

Feature Engineering
Translating raw data into the signals a lender actually uses

When an experienced credit officer reviews a loan application, they are implicitly asking three questions. The model asks the same questions, but systematically and at scale across every application.

AffordabilityCan this person sustain the repayments given their income and existing obligations?
ANNUITY_TO_INCOME
AMT_ANNUITY / AMT_INCOME
Monthly repayment as a share of annual income. Values above 30% indicate meaningful affordability stress. This is the single strongest predictor in the model.
CREDIT_TO_INCOME
AMT_CREDIT / AMT_INCOME
Loan amount as a multiple of annual income. A loan worth 8 years of income is a fundamentally different risk from one worth 1 year, even if the monthly repayment looks similar.
BUREAU_DEBT_TO_INCOME
BUREAU_TOTAL_DEBT / AMT_INCOME
Total debt across all lenders relative to income. Looking at this loan in isolation misses the full picture. An applicant may look affordable here but be stretched across multiple obligations elsewhere.
LOAN_COST_RATIO
(ANNUITY x TERM) / CREDIT
Total repayments divided by the original loan. Captures the interest burden that monthly repayment alone misses: the same monthly payment over 48 months costs far more in total than over 24.
StabilityHow reliable and predictable is this person's financial situation?
AGE_YEARS
-DAYS_BIRTH / 365
Younger applicants have shorter employment histories, less savings cushion, and more volatile income trajectories. Age is a proxy for financial maturity and resilience to shocks.
EMPLOYMENT_YEARS
-DAYS_EMPLOYED / 365
Length of current employment directly signals income reliability. Someone employed for ten or more years is far less likely to face sudden income disruption than someone who started recently.
Credit HistoryWhat does this person's past behaviour tell us about their future behaviour?
BUREAU_TOTAL_DPD
DPD_30 + DPD_60 + DPD_90
Total count of all historical late payment events. The single most reliable behavioural signal: someone who has missed payments before is significantly more likely to miss them again.
EXT_SOURCE_MEAN
mean(EXT_1, EXT_2, EXT_3)
Average of three independent external credit bureau scores. Each bureau looks at the applicant from a different angle. Combining them produces a more reliable signal than any single source.
EXT_SOURCE_MIN
min(EXT_1, EXT_2, EXT_3)
The weakest external score. The average can mask a red flag: if one agency rates an applicant very low, that signal matters regardless of what the other two say.
BUREAU_UTILIZATION
BUREAU_UTILIZATION (0 if missing)
Credit utilisation across existing accounts. High utilisation indicates existing credit lines are nearly exhausted, which is a leading indicator of financial stress.

Preprocessing Pipeline
Ensuring the model is evaluated honestly

Stratified Train / Validation Split

The 50,000 applicants are split 80/20 into a training set (40,000) and a validation set (10,000). The split is stratified on TARGET, meaning both sets preserve the 8.1% default rate. This is important: if the validation set had a different default rate, the performance metrics would not be comparable to the real portfolio.

SplitSizeDefault RatePurpose
Training set40,0008.1%Model learns from this data. All preprocessing fitted here.
Validation set10,0008.1%Model is evaluated on this data. Never seen during training.

Why Preprocessing Must Be Fitted Only on Training Data

Preprocessing steps such as filling missing values with the median income require computing statistics from the data. If those statistics are computed on all 50,000 rows before splitting, the validation set's values have already influenced the preprocessing. The model is then tested on data that has already shaped its inputs and the evaluation is no longer independent.

Wrong Causes Data Leakage
Compute imputation statistics on all 50,000 rows, then split. The validation set influenced the parameters the model uses. Performance metrics are optimistic and will not reflect real-world accuracy on new applicants.
Correct No Leakage
Split first. Compute statistics on the 40,000 training rows only. Apply those same parameters to both sets. The validation set never influenced any parameter. The evaluation is honest.

This is enforced automatically by scikit-learn's Pipeline: fit() sees only training data, and transform() applies learned parameters to both sets without re-fitting.

Model Training and Evaluation
How the model works and how well it performs

Model Selection: Logistic Regression

Logistic Regression assigns a weight to each feature, sums the weighted values, and converts the result to a probability between 0 and 1. It is the industry standard baseline for credit scoring because its weights are directly interpretable every decision can be explained in terms of specific input values and it is the format most familiar to regulators and credit risk practitioners.

ANNUITY_TO_INCOME has the largest positive coefficient by a wide margin, confirming that repayment burden relative to income is the dominant risk driver. EXT_SOURCE_MEAN is negative, confirming that higher external bureau scores reduce default probability. EXT_SOURCE_2 shows a counterintuitive positive coefficient. This is a known statistical issue called multicollinearity: when three highly correlated variables (EXT_SOURCE_1, 2, and 3) are included together, their individual coefficients become unstable. The composite EXT_SOURCE_MEAN correctly shows a negative coefficient and is the variable to use when explaining decisions to applicants or regulators.

From Coefficients to SHAP

Coefficients show the model's fixed weights, the same for every applicant. In practice, the impact of a feature depends on the applicant's actual value. A high ANNUITY_TO_INCOME coefficient matters a great deal for someone whose repayments consume 70% of their income, but much less for someone at 10%. SHAP in Section 5 translates this into individual-level explanations for any specific applicant.

Risk Bands and Lending Policy
Translating model scores into decisions the lending team can act on

A probability score between 0 and 1 is not a lending decision. The final step translates scores into three actionable tracks. Thresholds are set at the 30th and 60th percentile of predicted scores, producing a 30/30/40 portfolio split. The Auto Approve threshold was chosen because it produces a band with a 0.8% actual default rate, which sits within standard retail credit risk appetite. The Decline threshold was chosen because above it, the actual default rate of 18.4% significantly exceeds what the portfolio can absorb. Both thresholds are validated against real outcomes from the 10,000 held-out applicants.

What Underwriters Should Focus On in Manual Review

Applicants in the Manual Review band are not borderline bad risks. They are applicants where the model identifies uncertainty. An underwriter adds value here by assessing three things the model cannot determine from application data alone: whether the stated income is verifiable through payslips or bank statements, whether historical delinquencies reflect a pattern or an isolated event such as a medical emergency, and whether the loan amount and purpose are consistent with the applicant's overall financial profile.

Policy Rationale

Auto Approve (bottom 30%): At a 0.8% actual default rate, this group sits within standard retail credit risk appetite. Straight-through processing reduces cost and improves the customer experience. No underwriter time should be spent here.

Manual Review (middle 30%): The model identifies material but not disqualifying risk. Human judgment adds genuine value: income verification, context around delinquency history, and assessment of whether a reduced loan amount would bring the risk into an acceptable range.

Decline (top 40%): At an 18.4% default rate, the expected loss from approving this group exceeds what standard risk appetite would allow. Applicants at the lower end of this band, where PD is close to 26.2%, may be worth reconsidering for a smaller secured product rather than an outright decline.

Why You Can Trust This Model
Three questions any lending model must answer before deployment

A model's performance on training data means nothing. What matters is whether it works on applicants it has never seen, whether its decisions align with sound credit judgment, and whether someone will notice if it starts to fail.

✅
Does it work on unseen applicants?
The model was evaluated on 10,000 applicants it never saw during training. The Decline band actual default rate of 18.4% compared to the Approve band's 0.8% is validated on real outcomes. The 23x separation between bands confirms the model is genuinely discriminating between risk levels, not fitting noise.
🧠
Do its decisions make sense?
The model's top risk drivers are repayment burden relative to income, historical missed payments, and external credit bureau scores. These align with what experienced credit officers look at, which gives confidence that the model is capturing genuine credit signals rather than statistical patterns that happen to correlate with defaults in this dataset.
📊
How will we know if it stops working?
Check the actual default rate in each band every quarter. If the Approve band's rate rises above 2%, the model is letting through too much risk. If the Decline band's rate drops well below 18%, the model may be becoming too conservative. Any lending manager can track these two numbers without needing to understand the model itself.
Recommended Monitoring Approach
Review actual default rates within each band quarterly against the baselines established here: 0.8% for Auto Approve, 1.7% for Manual Review, and 18.4% for Decline. A deviation of more than 3 percentage points in any band warrants investigation. Retrain the model annually on the most recent 24 months of data to ensure it reflects current applicant behaviour. If the credit environment changes significantly, such as a material shift in interest rates or employment conditions, trigger an earlier review.
